History




Many studies assert that origami was invented by the Japanese about a thousand of years ago, but its root may be well in China. It is also highly probable that the process of folding was applied to other materials before paper was invented, so the origins of recreational folding may lie with cloth or leather. Origami started in the 17th century in Japan. By the mid-1900s, it had become a popular art form worldwide. It is an ancient and beautiful art of paper folding, creating varied designs such as animals and objects, like the popular swan or boat shapes. The name origami stems from the Japanese language of "ori" meaning fold and "gami" meaning paper. It is thought that origami originally became the part of Japanese culture when Bhuddhist monks brought paper which is an expensive luxury item at that time, to Japan in the 6th century, where it was used during religious ceremonies. The techniques of origami can create 3D structures and mechanisms from 2D planar sheets. The forms and functions of 3D origami products are determined by their underlying creasus and are thus created using different folding patterns. The first Japanese origami was used for religious ceremonial purposes only, due to high price of paper.

Facts


Origami came to Japan thanks to the Bhuddhist monks. Folidng of 1,000 paper cranes is believed to fulfill wishes. It is famous in spain too. The goal is to transform a flat square sheet of paper into a finished sculpture through folding and sculpting techniques. The earliest known traditions of Japanese paper folding were of ceremonial origin, such as the Japanese Noshi which is the white paper folded with a strip of dried abalone or meat, attached to the gifts and considered a token of good fortune. The origami was first known as the "orikata" or "folded shapes". In the year 1880, the name was changed to "origami" or "folded shapes".


Modern Era




The modern growth of interest in origami dates to the design in the year 1954 by Akira Yoshizawa of a notation to indicate how to fold origami models. The Yoshizawa-Randlett system is now used internationally. Today the popularity of origami societies such as the British Origami Society and Origami USA. The origami rules for the ancient origami were less strict, the formula for modern origami always requires a square sheet of paper with no cuts, glue or markings during the folding process. With even a small number of basic folds anyone can create intricate designs. Origami inspired engineering has been used in NASA space equipment, in the development of better heart stents, and even in micro-biology, with folding of DNA. In recent years, PBS - Public Broadcast System has featured two documentaries on the scientific uses of origami. Akira Yoshizawa was born in the year 1911 and he is known as the father of modern origami. The publication of his first collection of models in the early 1950s caused a major sensation. In 1954, he founded the International Origami Centre in Tokyo. The origami helps children to develop patience, concentration power and self-esteem. Cooperative learning - origami is a well-suited activity to do with the whole classroom. In a multi-age classroom, paper folding tends to eliminate the status associated with age differences.
